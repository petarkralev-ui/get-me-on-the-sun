The mortgage is
easier than you think.

As a non-resident, the process is fully open to you — but a few specific steps trip almost everyone up. Here's how to skip past them.

What Actually Slows You Down

The five things that decide how smooth this goes

🪪

Get your NIE in person, early

The NIE (your foreigner ID number) is required for almost every step that follows — the mortgage, the notary, even opening a bank account. The paperwork side of this was, by far, the biggest source of delay in the whole process. The most reliable route is applying in person at a police station in Spain, rather than going through a consulate abroad, where wait times can add weeks. We can also book your appointment for you and arrange for a Spanish guide and translator to attend with you, so no time is wasted. We keep up with all the documents needed too, so you don't have to.

🤝

Use a mortgage broker — it's genuinely worth it

Trying to navigate Spanish mortgage bureaucracy alone, from abroad, without fluent Spanish, is a false economy. A broker who works with non-residents already knows which banks say yes, what documents they'll ask for, and how to package your application so it doesn't stall. This alone turned a confusing process into a manageable one.

🏦

A good bank agent changes the outcome

Beyond the broker, having a bank-side contact who makes sure your file is complete — and who pushes for a lower repayment rate — makes a real difference to both approval speed and the interest rate you're offered. This relationship is worth investing time in before you formally apply.

📊

Expect 60–70% loan-to-value

As a non-resident, Spanish banks typically finance somewhere in the 60–70% range of the property's value — not the higher percentages sometimes available to residents. Plan your deposit around this from the start, so it doesn't become a surprise midway through.

🌐

Language is not a barrier

If you don't speak Spanish, that's not a reason to hesitate. English- and Russian-speaking staff are available who can lead you through the entire process, from first enquiry to signing at the notary.

⚠ The trap most buyers don't see coming

The real risk isn't the mortgage — it's the contract with the seller's agency. When you agree a price and sign with an agency, the contract terms determine what happens if the bank's official valuation later comes in below that price.

Some contracts are written in the buyer's favor and protect your deposit if this happens. Others aren't — and if the valuation falls short and your contract doesn't cover you, you can lose your deposit entirely, which can run into the thousands.

The fix: don't rely solely on the bank's own valuator. Working with a private, independent valuator — one that works in your favor rather than the lender's — and making sure your purchase contract has the right protection clauses before you sign, is what actually protects your money here.
