The Regime

What the ZEC actually gives you

💼

A 4% corporate tax rate

Companies registered under the ZEC (Zona Especial Canaria) pay a reduced 4% Corporate Income Tax rate on qualifying activity, instead of Spain's general rate of around 25%. This applies up to a capped tax base, which scales with the number of jobs the company creates.

🌍

Treaty benefits reach well beyond the UK

Dividends, interest, and capital gains paid out to parent companies are exempt from withholding tax where an applicable treaty exists — and Spain has double-taxation agreements with more than fifty countries, plus the EU parent-subsidiary directive. This isn't a UK-specific arrangement; it applies broadly across the EU and beyond, depending on your home country's treaty with Spain.

Qualifying

Who actually qualifies

The ZEC is a genuine business regime, not an automatic perk of owning property. To register a company under it, you generally need to:

01

Set up a new company

Be a newly created entity (or branch) with its registered office and real management based in the Canary Islands — with at least one administrator resident there.

02

Invest a minimum amount

Make a minimum investment of roughly €100,000 in Tenerife or Gran Canaria (or €50,000 on the other islands) in business assets, within the first two years of registration.

03

Create local jobs

Create at least 5 jobs (Tenerife/Gran Canaria) or 3 jobs (other islands) within six months of registering, and maintain that average while you hold the benefit.

04

Run a permitted activity

Operate within the list of activities the ZEC actually authorizes. A handful of sectors — including traditional primary industry, construction, and financial intermediation — are excluded.

Important — read before assuming this applies to you

Buying a personal property in Tenerife does not, by itself, qualify you for ZEC benefits. The ZEC is designed for companies running a real economic activity — with genuine investment and job creation — not simply for holding a house.

Where it can become relevant is if you set up a company to run your property as an actual rental business, structured to meet the investment and employment thresholds above. That's a real strategy, but it's a deliberate business decision, not something that happens automatically when you sign at the notary.

Before assuming any of this applies to your situation, get advice from a qualified tax advisor who can look at your specific circumstances — the requirements and thresholds above are a general guide, not a substitute for professional advice.
